Tōkyō Teleport Town

As early as 1985, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government was intending to utilize the reclaimed land in Tokyo Bay to create space that would help decentralize the busy city into many smaller subcentres. In 1994, plans were laid for the futuristic area of Tōkyō Teleport Town, which would be made up of four districts: Aomi, Ariake Minami, Ariake Kita and Daiba. The World Exposition Tokyo '96 was planned to bring life to the area. However, when new governor Aoshima Yukio came to power in 1995, the Expo was scrapped, and for a while there was concern that the reclaimed bay area was going to be a huge financial dead zone after all. (Until this point Odaiba was entirely an industrial zone and had only recently been rezoned.) Fortunately, even without the Expo's help, the area managed to develop as a tourism and leisure spot. Aside from the Rinkai-sen's Tōkyō Teleport Station and the Teleportation Bridge overpass that stretches from Odaiba-kaihinkōen to Palette Town, though, the Tōkyō Teleport Town name didn't stick.

Another consequence of the Expo cancellation was the slowdown of development in order to be more in line with the then-unstable economy, and the permitting of more varied land uses for amusement facilities. Odaiba's development period was extended another 10 years to compensate. Sometime prior to 1998, the Tōkyō Teleport Town moniker was changed to Rainbow Town, a name which does not yet appear to have worked out any better than the first. People continue to refer to the area at large as simply "Odaiba."
